# Objection Handling

## General Principles

- **Listen completely** without interrupting
- **94% of sales aren't lost on price** (Brian Tracy). Most price objections hide something else
- Distinguish **conditions** (unchangeable facts) from **objections** (resolvable concerns)
- Treat every objection as a **request for more information**
- Never take an objection personally
- Cardone's first rule: **"ALWAYS, ALWAYS, ALWAYS AGREE"**

## "I don't have time" / "I'm too busy"

**Mirroring (Voss):**
> "Sounds like you're swamped with work..." [pause, let them elaborate]

**Time-Bound Hook:**
> "Totally understand. Give me just 30 seconds to explain why I'm calling, and if it doesn't seem relevant, we hang up. No problem."

**Reschedule with Commitment:**
> "I respect your time 100%. If I could show you how to save $X/month, would it be worth 15 minutes? What day works for you?"

## "It's too expensive"

**Isolate the Objection:**
> "I understand. When you say expensive, is it that it's outside your budget, or that you're not sure the return justifies the investment?"

**Reframe Cost vs Price:**
> "How much do you pay now in [fees/commissions/lost time]? Because most of our clients recoup the cost in the first month just from what they save."

**Make Big Numbers Small:**
> "$X/month sounds like a lot, but that's less than $Y/day. Less than a coffee. And with that you get [full solution]."

**Too Expensive Compared to What:**
> "Too expensive compared to what? To what you're paying now? To another solution? Help me understand your reference point."

## "I already use another solution" (Competitor)

**Discovery:**
> "Great that you're already in the game. Is it working well? Anything you wish was different?"

**Acknowledge + Differentiate:**
> "[Competitor] is a good tool. What happens is that many of our clients came from there and told us they [specific pain point]. Has that happened to you?"

**Don't Replace, Complement:**
> "I'm not asking you to drop [Competitor] today. Just inviting you to see how it works and compare for yourself. The decision is yours."

## "I'm not interested"

**Mirror + Silence (Voss):**
> "Not interested..." [statement with slight upward inflection, then SILENCE]. They almost always elaborate WHY they're not interested, giving you the real objection.

**Pattern Interrupt:**
> "I understand. I'm not asking for a decision today. I'm just calling because we help [type of business] achieve [concrete result]. If it's not on your radar, no problem."

**Label the Emotion (Voss):**
> "Sounds like you've been called a thousand times by people trying to sell you something... and I don't blame you."

**Value Statement:**
> "Usually when someone tells me that, it's because I haven't shown them how [competitors/peers] achieved [result]. Give me 2 minutes and if it doesn't seem relevant, I won't call again."

## "Send me info by email"

**Honest Call-Out:**
> "Happy to. But let me be honest: usually when someone says that it's a polite way of saying 'I'm not interested.' If that's the case, no offense taken. But if you're genuinely interested, 5 minutes of conversation is more effective than a 20-page PDF."

**Qualify Before Sending:**
> "Sure. I have a 50-page deck and a 2-page summary. What specifically would you want to know? That way I send you something relevant."

**Schedule the Follow-Up:**
> "Perfect, I'll send the info now. So it doesn't get lost in your inbox, let's schedule 10 minutes on [day] to address questions?"

## "I need to think about it"

**Discover the Real Objection:**
> "Totally, it's an important decision. When you say you need to think about it, what specifically is giving you pause? Is it the price? The implementation?"

**Calibrated Question (Voss):**
> "What would need to happen for you to feel comfortable making this decision?"

**"Think About What" Technique:**
> "Sure. What specific part do you want to think about? That way when we talk I can bring you exactly what you need."

**Soft Close with Timeline:**
> "Perfect. When do you think you'll have a decision? Let's talk on [date]?"

## "I don't have the budget"

**Isolate Value vs Budget:**
> "I understand. If budget wasn't an issue, is this something you'd be interested in?" (If they say YES = budget problem. If they say NO = value problem)

**Budget Displacement:**
> "Is there a tool you're paying for right now that's not delivering results? Many clients free up budget by cutting what [Product] replaces."

**Cost of Inaction:**
> "If the problem costs you $X/month, every month you go without solving it is money lost. How much longer can you wait?"

**Future Commitment:**
> "When does your next budget cycle start? I'd love to schedule something for that time."

## Feel, Felt, Found Method (Brian Tracy)

1. **FEEL** -- Validate their emotion: "I understand how you feel..."
2. **FELT** -- Social proof: "Many of our clients felt the same way..."
3. **FOUND** -- Resolution: "What they found was..."

**Price example:**
> "I understand how you feel about the price. Many of our most successful clients felt the same way at first. But what they found was that in the first 3 months they recouped the investment because they stopped paying [fees/commissions/overhead]."

**Platform switch example:**
> "I understand your concern. Several clients felt the same -- they were afraid to switch. What they found was that the process took less than a week and their customers noticed the improvement immediately."

## Quick Reference: Which Technique by Objection

| Objection | Primary Technique | Support Technique |
|---|---|---|
| I don't have time | Pattern interrupt + time-bound hook | Mirroring (Voss) |
| It's too expensive | Reframe cost vs price | Social proof + COI |
| Already use competitor | Discovery questions + differentiation | Feel-Felt-Found |
| Not interested | Mirror + silence | Accusation audit |
| Send me info | Honest call-out (push/pull) | Commitment & consistency |
| Need to think about it | Calibrated question | 1-10 close |
| No budget | Isolate value vs budget | Cost of inaction |
