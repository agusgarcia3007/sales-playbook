# Closing Techniques & Follow-Up

## CLOSING TECHNIQUES

The average salesperson asks for the sale only once. Top performers ask 5+ times using different closing techniques (Brian Tracy). The key: each attempt uses a different angle, not the same question repeated.

### Assumptive Close
Act as if the sale is already decided. Skip "Do you want to buy?" and go straight to implementation.
> "Perfect. So the next step would be to set up your account and schedule onboarding. Does Monday or Wednesday work better?"

### Summary Close
Restate all agreed-upon value points before asking for commitment.
> "So: you need [requirement 1], [requirement 2], and [requirement 3]. We saw that [Product] does exactly that and saves you $X/month. The only step left is..."

### Puppy Dog Close (Trial Close)
Let the product sell itself through experience. Conversion rates from trial to paid average 25-40% for well-qualified prospects.
> "I understand it's an important decision. How about we give you access for 14 days, no credit card, and your team tests it? If it works, we continue. If not, no commitment."

### Alternative Close
Give two options — both result in a sale. Removes the "yes/no" binary and replaces with "which one."
> "We have two plans: the Pro with up to 500 users, or the Business with unlimited users and priority support. Which fits better?"

### 1-10 Close
Measure commitment level and address the gap. If they say anything above 5, you're in good shape.
> "On a scale of 1 to 10, where 10 is 'ready to start today', where do you feel?"
> If they say 7: "What would need to happen to take you from 7 to 10?"

### Sharp Angle Close
When they ask for a concession, use it as closing leverage. Works because you're giving them what they want in exchange for commitment.
> Prospect: "Could you include personalized onboarding?"
> You: "If I include onboarding, can we start this week?"

### Next Step Close
The softest close. Simply outline what happens next. Reduces friction by making the process feel easy.
> "Next steps: first I send you access, which takes 5 minutes to set up. Then we schedule your onboarding. By Monday your account is live. Sound good?"

### Ben Franklin Close
The prospect lists reasons to buy on one side and reasons not to on the other. Help them fill the "for" column. Works for analytical buyers who need to rationalize the decision.

### Doorknob Close
When you're leaving, turn and say: "Oh, by the way..." and introduce one last compelling point when defenses are down. Works because the formal "selling" moment has passed.

### Invitational Close (Brian Tracy)
Simply invite: "Why not give it a try?" or "Which package would you like?" Works because it's low-pressure and assumes goodwill.

### Cost of Inaction Close
Quantify the cost of doing nothing. Deals close 40% faster when the prospect understands the cost of delay.
> "Let's do the math: if this problem costs you $X/month, every month you wait is money lost. In the 3 months you think about it, that's $Y gone."

### Cardinal Rule of Closing (Brian Tracy)
> **"The only pressure allowed in a professional sales presentation is the pressure of silence after the closing question."**

Ask. Then SHUT UP. The first person to speak loses. Average reps wait 2 seconds. Top reps wait 8+.

### TDPPR Formula for Post-Meeting Follow-Up (Brian Tracy)
Never leave a meeting without scheduling the next one:
- **T** — Time
- **D** — Date
- **P** — Place/platform
- **P** — Participants
- **R** — Reason for the meeting

## FOLLOW-UP SEQUENCE

### Key Statistics
- 55% of replies come from follow-ups, not the initial email
- 48% of salespeople never follow up even once
- Campaigns with 4-7 emails achieve 27% reply rate — 3x more than 1-3 email sequences (Woodpecker, 20M emails)
- By 4th follow-up, response rates drop 55% and spam complaints triple — cap at 4-5 follow-ups total
- First follow-up adds 49% more replies vs stopping at initial email

### Optimal Cadence

Increase the gap between each touch:

| Touch | Day | Channel | Angle |
|-------|-----|---------|-------|
| Initial | 0 | Email or WhatsApp | Maximum personalization + core value prop + soft CTA |
| Follow-up 1 | 3 | Email | Different angle, new value piece (stat, insight, resource) |
| Follow-up 2 | 7 | WhatsApp | Social proof / case study from similar company |
| Follow-up 3 | 14 | Email | New insight, industry trend, or relevant resource |
| Follow-up 4 | 21 | WhatsApp | Acknowledge silence, offer value, respect boundaries |
| Follow-up 5 | 30 | Email | **Breakup email** — triggers loss aversion |

### Angle Rotation

Each follow-up must stand alone and add genuinely new value. Never "just checking in."

Add only **one new value proposition per email**. This naturally forces different angles:
- Email 1: Problem + solution
- Email 2: Different benefit or data point
- Email 3: Case study / social proof
- Email 4: Industry insight or resource
- Email 5: Breakup — remove pressure, create scarcity through withdrawal

### The Breakup Email

Generates the highest response rate of the entire sequence — 10-15% response rates with cold prospects (Close.com). Works through loss aversion: removing pressure while creating scarcity through withdrawal.

**Standard format:**
```
Subject: Should I close your file?

Hi [Name],

I've written a couple of times and haven't heard back,
which usually means one of three things:

1. Interested but not the right time — happy to reach
   out in a few months
2. Already solved it — great, I'll stop writing
3. It slipped through — reply "interested" and I'll
   send the details

I don't want to fill your inbox. Let me know which one.

[Your name]
```

**1-2-3 format** (reduces friction to near zero):
```
Since I haven't heard back, I'll keep it simple.
Reply with a number:

1 — Interested, let's talk
2 — Not now, check back in 3 months
3 — Not interested, please stop
```

**Critical rule:** If you send a breakup email, honor it. Do not contact the prospect again unless they reply.

### Phrases That Kill Response Rates

| Phrase | Impact |
|---|---|
| "I never heard back" | -12% meeting bookings (Gong) |
| "Just checking in" | Zero value, signals laziness |
| "Bumping this up" | Presumptuous |
| "Did you see my last email?" | Guilt-tripping |
| "Following up on my previous message" | Generic, adds nothing |

### Timing Rules
- **Best days:** Tuesday-Thursday. Thursday peaks at 6.87% reply rate
- **Best times:** 9-11 AM or 1-3 PM in prospect's local timezone
- **Avoid:** Monday mornings (inbox overload), Friday afternoons (checked out)
- **SMB:** 5-8 touchpoints in 30 days
- **Enterprise:** 10-18 touchpoints in 30-60 days
- **Multi-channel:** Limit emails to 50% of total touches. Mix WhatsApp, calls, voice notes

### CTA Adjustment by Seniority

- **Executives/founders:** Ultra-low-effort, curiosity-driven. "Curious?" or "Worth 2 min?"
- **Mid-level managers:** More specific value. "Want me to walk through how [Company] saved X?"
- **Technical buyers:** Data-driven, precise. "Here's the ROI calculator — want to run your numbers?"

Higher in the org chart = less friction you can ask for.
